# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Network, DevOps, infrastructure, cloud, and data center engineers. They are technical professionals working inside corporate environments. They use TopoForge during active infrastructure documentation work — converting existing LLD spreadsheets into publishable HLD diagrams. The context is focused, task-oriented, and usually on a workstation at a desk.

## Product Purpose

TopoForge eliminates the manual redraw step in network documentation. An engineer uploads a structured LLD spreadsheet, reviews and corrects the parsed topology, answers clarification questions, and downloads a clean editable Draw.io HLD diagram. Success means the engineer spends zero time in a diagram editor for initial layout.

## Brand Personality

Calm, uncluttered, professional. The tool should feel like a well-maintained internal platform — authoritative without being loud, precise without being cold. No decorative complexity. Every element earns its place by helping the engineer complete the task.

## References

Linear — specifically its information density, muted neutral palette, confident typography, and the sense that the interface was built by people who actually use it. Not the consumer-SaaS warmth or growth-marketing gloss, but the engineering-tool restraint.

## Anti-references

Consumer SaaS dashboards — hero metrics, gradient accents, identical card grids, marketing-flavored copy. This is a professional engineering tool, not a product landing page. Also avoid: terminal-aesthetic darkness for its own sake, glassmorphism used decoratively, and anything that feels like it was designed to impress a VC rather than help an engineer work.

## Design Principles

1. **Task over decoration** — every visual element must serve the engineer's workflow. If it doesn't help them understand, correct, or act, remove it.
2. **Earned confidence** — the interface should feel authoritative because of its precision and clarity, not because of visual weight or dark chrome.
3. **Information at rest** — default states should be quiet. Alerts, emphasis, and color should surface only when the engineer needs to act.
4. **Linear density** — comfortable density with clear hierarchy. Not sparse and empty, not overwhelming. Engineers can read tables.
5. **Consistent, not clever** — the five wizard steps should feel like one coherent tool, not five individually designed screens.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Corporate standard: WCAG 2.1 AA. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Ensure sufficient contrast in both light and dark themes. All interactive elements must be keyboard accessible.
